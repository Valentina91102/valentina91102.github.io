Summer of Science

Experiment

My experiment tested the effectiveness of combining polyaluminum chloride (PAC), calcium peroxide (CaO2), and kaolinite clay to mitigate Microcystis blooms. PAC acted as a flocculant to aggregate Microcystis cells, while CaO2 released hydrogen peroxide and oxygen over time to inhibit Microcystis growth and toxin production. Kaolinite clay served as a ballast to enhance sedimentation of the aggregated cells and toxins. The study measured the impact of this combination on the photophysiology of *Microcystis* and microcystin levels, aiming to develop an environmentally friendly method to control harmful algal blooms.

Methods

The initial exposure tests used varying concentrations of CaO2 to determine the most effective dose, identifying 5 (mg/L) as optimal. Subsequent experiments tested combinations of CaO2, PAC, and kaolinite for flocculation and sedimentation in both small-scale (50 mL) and upscaled (125 mL) setups. Samples were taken at intervals (0, 2, 24, 48, and 72 hours) to measure cell abundance (via TD-700 Fluorometer), photosynthetic efficiency (using Pulse-Amplitude-Modulation (PAM) Fluorometry), and toxin levels through Enzyme-linked immunosorbent assay (ELISA). Analyses focused on evaluating the treatments' effects on Microcystis biomass, photophysiology, and microcystin production.
